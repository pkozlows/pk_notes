\section{Introduction}
The consensus is that the best way to adapt to climate change is to mitigate it by reducing greenhouse gas emissions \cite{OECD2022-vn}. To do so requires the development of next-generation green technologies, such as electrocatalysts\cite{Seh2017-jx}, photovoltaics\cite{Du2023-kd}, and batteries\cite{Titirici2024-ao}. These seemingly disparate goals can all be achieved through the discovery of materials with special properties. Spectroscopy is an indispensable tool for experimentalists in the search for such properties, as it illuminates the electronic structure of candidate materials. Computation is crucial for interpreting spectroscopic data, and thus quantum chemists devote much effort to the accurate and efficient simulation of spectra\cite{Neese2025-lp}.

Spectroscopy occurs when incoming light perturbs matter, a process which proceeds through excited states of the system. The excited states of a material may be described in terms of quasiparticles, which are collective excitations of interacting electrons. The equation-of-motion coupled-cluster formalism including single and double excitations (EOM-CCSD) can accurately describe these quasiparticles \cite{Krylov2008-sg}, but it carries a computational scaling of $O(N^6)$, where $N$ is the system size. This is to be compared with semi-local density functional theory's (DFT) $O(N^3)$ scaling, so it comes as no surprise that DFT is the method of choice for large systems\cite{Teale2022-eu}. However, DFT is known to be inaccurate for describing excited states\cite{Cohen2012-uv}, so there is a need for methods that are more accurate than DFT but more computationally tractable than EOM-CCSD.

A compelling alternative is presented by many-body perturbation theory (MBPT)\cite{Golze2019-wm}. MBPT introduces a variety of "gap-filling" methods, in the sense that these methods provide a more accurate description of excited states than DFT while remaining more computationally tractable than EOM-CCSD.


\section{Excited states via MBPT}
The native language of MBPT is that of Green's functions, which are used to describe the propagation of quasiparticles. The exact propagator is given by the interacting Green's function, $G$. The reference propagator is given by the noninteracting Green's function, $G_0$. The exact self-energy $\Sigma$ captures the difference between the two. However, solving for the exact quantities is computationally intractable, so approximations are necessary.

\subsection{Quasiparticle Energies from GW}
GW is a widely used approximation to the self-energy, which gets its name from postulating that $\Sigma$ is the product of the Green's function $G$ and the screened Coulomb interaction $W$ in the time domain, i.e. $\Sigma^{GW}(t) = i G(t) W(t)$. The GW method has been shown to give quasiparticle energies rivaling EOM-CCSD \textit{(cite)}. Formally, GW carries a computational scaling of $O(N^6)$, but in practice analytic continuation (AC) is used to reduce the scaling to $O(N^4)$. However, GW with AC does a poor job describing quasiparticle energies of core states, which may be important for accurately describing the system.

Via a Löwdin partitioning technique, Loos et al. showed*\cite{Quintero-Monsebaiz2022-en}* that one can attain the exact quasiparticle energies within the GW approximation by either solving a nonlinear quasiparticle equation, as is traditionally done, or by determining the eigenvalues of an effective Hamiltonian, which we denote $\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{GW}}$. However, an explicit construction of $\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{GW}}$ carries the same $O(N^6)$ cost as solving the exact GW quasiparticle equation.

\subsubsection{Moment-conserving GW (MCGW)}
Booth et al. \cite{Scott2023-gc} claimed to have resolved this issue in 2023, by reformulating $\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{GW}}$ in terms of the spectral moments of the self-energy. Considering self-energy moments to infinite order would be equivalent to constructing the full $\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{GW}}$, but it is argued that truncating the moment expansion to some finite order yields a good approximation. A procedure inspired by the Lanczos algorithm is devised to iteratively diagonalize $\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{GW}}$, with the Ritz values expected to rapidly converge to the ED solution as the number of moments increases. If one uses the resolution-of-the-identity (RI) approximation to the electron repulsion integrals, no step in this procedure scales higher than $O(N^4)$.

However, in my implementation, I found that the Ritz values do not always converge to the exact quasiparticle energies of ED. The Lanczos algorithm is a well-established numerical method\textit{cite} whose Ritz values are guaranteed to converge to the exact diagonalization (ED) under proper conditions, given enough iterations to proceed. However, one of these conditions requires that orthogonality is maintained in the Krylov subspace, typically done via periodic reorthogonalization.

Figure~\ref{fig:mcgw_failure} shows, for a number of small molecules, that MCGW initially follows the expected Lanczos convergence behavior towards ED, but then diverges. Analysis reveals that MCGW does not have access to its own Krylov subspace and therefore cannot perform reorthogonalization, leading to numerical instability. Therefore, it is misleading to associate MCGW with Lanczos. A reliable quantum chemistry method should remain faithful to its theoretical foundation without ambiguity from numerical artifacts, ensuring that errors remain below other dominant sources such as basis set incompleteness and grid effects \textit{cite}.

\subsection{Spectral function of the uniform electron gas (UEG)} The UEG is the paradigmatic metallic system \textit{cite}, serving as a testbed for many-body methods hoping to describe metals. Its exact spectral function in the thermodynamic limit is not known. Green's function methods are well suited for computing the spectral function $A(\omega)$, since it is  proportional to the imaginary part of the retarded Green's function: \begin{align} A(\omega) = -\frac{1}{\pi} \mathrm{Im}, G^R(\omega). \end{align}

Now, the retarded Green's function satisfies the Dyson equation: \begin{align} G^R(\omega) = G_0^R(\omega) + G_0^R(\omega) \Sigma^R(\omega) G^R(\omega). \end{align}

Because obtaining the exact self-energy is intractable, MBPT methods approximate $\Sigma$ through a perturbative expansion, with the interaction strength as the perturbation parameter. The GW approximation corresponds to choosing the parameter as the screened Coulomb interaction $W$ and truncating at first order in the perturbation series. This is equivalent to inserting $\Sigma^{GW}$ into this equation. However, solving the Dyson equation through truncation at first order fails to capture satellite features in core-level spectral functions of the UEG \textit{cite}.

In contrast, the cumulant expansion can reproduce these features. It expresses the Green's function as \begin{align} G^R(t) = G_0^R(t) e^{C^R(t)}, \end{align} where the cumulant is given by \begin{align} C^R(t) = \int d\omega \frac{\mathrm{Im}, \Sigma^R(\omega)}{\pi \omega^2} \left(e^{-i\omega t} + i \omega t -1\right). \end{align}

\subsubsection{$G_0@HF;C(GW)$} Initial cumulant approaches used $\Sigma^{GW}$ to construct the cumulant \textit{(cite)}. While this produces satellite features, their positions are often inaccurate relative to EOM-CCSD references \textit{(cite)}.

\subsubsection{$G_0@HF;C(PT2)$} Second-order perturbation theory (PT2) provides a second-order self-energy which can be used to build a cumulant. However, in metallic systems like the UEG, PT2 diverges in the thermodynamic limit, rendering this approach void.

\subsubsection{$G_0@BWs2;C(BWs2)$}
These issues may be mitigated by regularizing the second-order self-energy. Head-Gordon and co-workers recently developed a size-consistent Brillouin–Wigner second-order perturbation theory (BWs2) method, which scales as $O(N^5)$.

Recent work has shown that the BWs2 self-energy corresponds to a Goldstone self-energy, implying that it satisfies a Dyson-like equation with a dressed reference Green's function. This enables the construction of a BWs2-based cumulant:
\begin{equation}
C_{pp}^{\mathrm{BWs2}}(t) =
\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i a b}\frac{\langle p i | a b\rangle^2 }{\left(\tilde{\epsilon}*{p i}^{a b}\right)^2}\left(e^{-i \tilde{\epsilon}*{p i}^{a b} t} +i \tilde{\epsilon}*{p i}^{a b} t -1 \right)
+ \frac{1}{2}\sum*{i j a} \frac{\langle p a | i j\rangle^2}{\left(\tilde{\epsilon}*{p a}^{i j}\right)^2} \left(e^{-i \tilde{\epsilon}*{p a}^{i j} t} - 1 \right),
\end{equation}

where the energies are defined using BWs2-dressed orbital energies.

Preliminary results suggest that BWs2+C, scaling as $O(N^5)$, can improve upon GW+C without incurring the $O(N^6)$ cost of EOM-CCSD.

\section{Future directions}
\subsection{A self-consistent cumulant expansion}
Self-consistency has been incorporated into Dyson-based approaches with mixed success. These approaches solve the Dyson equation perturbatively, so adding self consistency may lead to the double counting of terms in the expansion. We propose exploring self-consistency within the cumulant framework, where diagrammatic consistency may be less restrictive.

\subsection{Memory kernel as an alternative to the self-energy}
The Nakajima–Zwanzig formalism leads to the generalized quantum master equation (GQME):
\begin{equation}
\dot{\mathcal{C}}(t) = \mathcal{C}(t) {\Omega_1} - \int_{0}^{t} d \tau, \mathcal{C}(t - \tau) \mathcal{K}_1(\tau) + D(t).
\end{equation}

This provides an alternative framework in which the memory kernel replaces the self-energy, potentially offering new routes for modeling excited-state dynamics.
